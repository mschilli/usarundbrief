@Z: Northern Exposure

<pic images/alaska-mountains.ps Brutally cold in winter: Fairbanks, Alaska>

<em>Michael</em> 
Angelika had the crazy idea to fly to icy cold Alaska for ten days in
the middle of winter. As preparation for the trip into the cold, we
borrowed all the episodes of "Northern Exposure" from the DVD rental
service Netflix. This TV series was dubbed in Germany in the 80s
under the title "Ausgerechnet Alaska." In the series, the state of
Alaska forces the newly graduated doctor Joel Fleischman to serve as
a village doctor in the fictional small town of Cicily for a few
years in order to pay off his student loans.

<pic images/northern-exposure-fleischman-office.ps The New York doctor with his Indian receptionist.>

The Jewish New Yorker Fleischman grumpily complies, then naturally
keeps complaining constantly like Woody Allen as a neurotic city dweller, but
eventually finds his way to the somewhat eccentric residents of the
isolated village. Ultimately, however, he remains an outsider in his
thick winter parka. Some ideas in the series are truly hilarious. For
example, some Native American inhabitants live in Cicily, who don't
talk much, form cliques, and celebrate the American holiday
"Thanksgiving" by throwing tomatoes at the white population.

<pic images/northern-exposure-men.ps "Men of the fictional small town 'Cicily' in the television series 'Northern Exposure'.>

The series has its highs, lows, and in the later seasons, some
considerably annoying lengths, but one episode is particularly worth
watching from a German perspective: When Maurice, a wealthy villager,
acquires a huge old German clock with a carillon and the auction
house flies in a specialist from Germany to get it up and running.

When
<LINK>http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z2pF5i3ZvYQ|Master watchmaker Rolf Hauser (Video)</LINK> arrives, he turns out to be a
leather jacket-wearing heavy metal fan with a Billy Idol look.

<pic images/northern-exposure-rolf-hauser.ps Rolf Hauser, a German master watchmaker with a Billy Idol look, explains the carillon clock to Maurice.>

The American actor Mark Pellegrino plays the role very convincingly
with an exaggerated German accent, covering pretty much all the
cliches that Americans attribute to Germans. The nicely edited
video under the aforementioned YouTube link is an indispensable
document of German-American cultural exchange. Definitely worth
watching!

@Z: By Train From Anchorage To Fairbanks

<pic images/alaska-train-door.ps The newsletter reporter boards the Polar Express.>

<em>Angelika</em> 
Train travel in America is known to be less suitable for getting from
point A to point B as quickly as possible, since the infrastructure
is built around cars and, for longer distances, airplanes. High-speed
trains like Germany's ICE or the Japanese Shinkansen are nowhere to be
found in the USA. Instead, train travel here is enveloped in an aura
of nostalgia. We therefore decided to take the train from Anchorage
to Fairbanks. The train route is considered one of the most beautiful
in Alaska, as the train passes by Denali National Park as a bonus. In
the depths of Alaska's winter, it runs only once a week and takes a
leisurely 12 hours to cover the 580 kms.

<pic images/alaska-train-tickets.ps At the ticket counter, things are moving along leisurely.>

In summer, by the way, it takes just as long. At first, I was a bit
skeptical about whether the train would be running reliably, because
Alaska has harsh winters, but the friendly lady from Alaska Railroad
assured me over the phone that in the four winters she has been
working for the railway company, the service had been only canceled once
due to bad weather. The train is pulled by robust diesel locomotives,
which are not easily affected by a bit of snow and cold.

In Anchorage, we checked into a hotel near the train station the night
before so that we could walk to the train the next morning. However,
this turned out to be more difficult than expected, as apparently no
one in Alaska considers it necessary to clear the streets and
sidewalks of snow. As a result, our rolling luggage occasionally got stuck in
the snow. We also had to arrive at the station an hour before
departure, to be granted our seat reservations, and check in our luggage.

<pic images/alaska-train-baggage.ps The luggage is placed in containers, which a railway employee brings to the train with a forklift.>

The luggage is placed in a separate luggage car on the train and is not
put on luggage racks above the seats. We were quite surprised
by how many people were waiting to board the train. The group was a
colorful mix: tourists and locals. Incidentally, residents of Alaska
receive a 20% discount when they use the Alaska Railroad. Many hotels
also offer Alaskans discounts, an interesting practice in an
otherwise capitalistic America. We particularly noticed a high
number of Japanese tourists, but Alaska in winter is a popular
honeymoon destination for Japanese people. This might surprise you,
but newlyweds come to see the Northern Lights. According to a
Japanese legend, children conceived under the Northern Lights will be
particularly successful in life. And Fairbanks, the final stop of the
train, is considered the Mecca of the Northern Lights. More on that
later.

<pic images/alaska-train-conductor.ps The conductor explains the procedure to people who have never traveled by train before and cracks funny jokes.>

In the waiting hall of the station, the conductor, who introduced
himself as "Steve," greeted all the train passengers. A veteran
with 30 years of service at Alaska Railroad, he gave us a brief
introduction to the upcoming journey. The man not only checked our
tickets but also entertained us during the train ride with all sorts
of anecdotes.

<pic images/alaska-train-interior.ps Not so great: The wagons are from the 1950s.>

Steve never tired of pointing out landmarks and roaming animals along the
side of the path. He had also set up a small personal museum on a
train seat. This included, in addition to a personal photo album with
all sorts of photos of various train routes, trains, his cabin in the
woods, and his children, real skulls of small animals. Steve also
knew the route like the back of his hand and knew exactly who lived
in which cabin along the track.

<pic images/alaska-train-trees.ps The sun rises at 10 o'clock in the morning.>

In Alaska, there are log cabins everywhere, where eccentric
outdoorsmen live in the wilderness. Often, these cabins can only be
reached on foot or in winter by snowmobile. By the way, Alaskans
refer to the wilderness as the "bush" and to the snowmobile as a
"snow machine," because "mobile" sounds like a mobile or a toy, and
therefore is probably not masculine enough, at least according to
conductor Steve.

About halfway up to Fairbanks, the train temporarily slowed to a snail's
pace because Steve had to drop off some newspapers for one of the
cabin residents near the railroad tracks. By the way, this particular
railroad is one of the few in the USA
that still offers a so-called "Flag Stop" service, meaning you can
stop the train in the middle of the route to get on or off by wildly
waving your arms or swinging a white T-shirt over your head. A white
flag works too (hence the name "Flag Stop"), but not everyone has one
readily available. However, this service is only available between
the towns of Talkeetna and Hurricane.

<pic images/alaska-train-hangout.ps Unruly passengers violate the regulations>

The train also offered several other tourist-friendly perks. For
example, there were exterior doors between the cars that were
split in two vertically, allowing the upper half to be opened for looking out
and taking photos. Interestingly, these doors are called "Dutch
Doors" in American English. However, we had to dress warmly for this,
as the wind from the moving train blew right through us.

<pic images/alaska-train-window.ps Angelika takes photographs at sub-zero temperatures from the open window of the moving train.>

<pic images/alaska-train-bagcar.ps The luggage car also travels with the door open so that people can take photographs.>

We were also allowed to go into the luggage car, which, like in
movies, traveled with an open door. As mentioned, this was only
possible because the train wasn't racing through the landscape at a
crazy speed. But, naturally, the passengers adapted to the more
leisurely pace. People were reading books or kept gazing out the window for hours to
admire the solitary winter landscape. In the dining car, board games
were brought out, and only rarely did someone use a cell phone, which
was also due to the fact that the reception along the route was only
moderate.

<pic images/alaska-fairbanks-smoke.ps Fairbanks, Alaska: City at the End of the World.>

When we arrived in Fairbanks, we were immediately hit by an icy cold,
as the place is much further north than Anchorage and is known for
its freezing winters. But conductor Steve had walked through the
train beforehand and ordered a taxi for everyone who needed one right
at the station, so we didn't have to stand in the cold for
long. That's what I call service.

@Z: Daylight

<pic images/alaska-lagoon.ps Fjord 50 miles south of Anchorage. The daylight is always slightly dim.>

<em>Angelika</em> 
Alaska is located near the Arctic Circle, which significantly affects
the daylight depending on the season: In the summer, it stays light
for an extremely long time, while in the winter, it remains dark for
a long period. In some places in Alaska (such as Barrow), the sun
doesn't set at all for several weeks in the summer, and conversely,
it won't rise at all in the winter. It's not quite as extreme in
Fairbanks, as the location is about 200 miles (approximately 320
kilometers) south of the Arctic Circle. On average, we had about six
hours of daylight, as the sun rises around 11 a.m. and sets again at
3 p.m. at the end of December, but there is also a longer twilight.
Around 10 a.m., the first light appears on the horizon, but at 9
a.m., it is still pitch dark. During the day, the light seems
somewhat strange because the sun never really rises high up in the sky.

<pic images/alaska-anchorage-downtown.ps Dark or not, Anchorage is Alaska's nightlife city, colloquially known as "Los Anchorage.>

Amazingly, we adapted very quickly to the local conditions, so we
were able to do and see a lot, despite the shorter days. The owner of
our bed-and-breakfast accommodation told us that during the summer
months, many locals completely exhaust themselves because they go
outside to exercise or work in the garden even at midnight, or simply
don't want to go to sleep because they want to fully enjoy the long
days.

<pic images/alaska-fairbanks-airport.ps Bone-dry snow at the Fairbanks airport that isn't even cleared.>

Most people we've met admitted that the cold in winter is not the problem, but rather
the darkness. The latter affects the mood after a few months, and
Alaskans have to compensate for the lack of vitamin D with vitamin
tablets. We heard some people in Fairbanks whispering that they had
already gained two more minutes of daylight today. The cold is indeed
more bearable in Fairbanks than elsewhere because it has a dry
climate without wind. The snow also looks particularly pretty because
it is completely powdery like desert sand and stays white for a long
time. However, snowmen or snowballs cannot be made from it.

<pic images/alaska-outfit.ps With this outfit, Michael reveals himself as a tourist, but even at minus 25 degrees, the three-layer high-tech material keeps him comfortably warm.>

<em>Michael</em> 
Native Alaskans would never wear a Gore-Tex jacket; instead, they
consistently dress in what I call the construction site look. They
prefer to order from online retailers like
<LINK>http://www.carhartt.com|Carhartt</LINK>,
offering work clothing for the various professional branches in the
fishing, oil platform welding, and truck driving industries.

When the clothes are completely worn out after 20 years and hang on
the body like rags, only then, they are suitable for Alaskans. They would never
go out on the street in a space-age manufactured, breathable,
possibly brightly colored garment, especially not in matching
outfits.

<pic images/isp-carpenters.ps Similar to these two trendsetters, the fashion-conscious Alaskan orders his clothes from the Carhartt catalog.>

When we were strolling along the sidewalk in Anchorage one evening,
bundled up against the freezing cold, a dented pickup truck actually
slowed down, and the passenger rolled down the window and shouted
that we were so pretty he just had to take a picture! Also, once, while
I was standing in the parking lot waiting for a taxi in my space
boots, which are rated for temperatures as low as minus 40 degrees, a
military-looking guy got out of his Jeep and slowly looked me up and
down. His gaze lingered on my shoes for several seconds. He probably
thought I really was visiting from outer space.

Call me Nostradamus. I hereby predict that European men will soon
also take a liking to construction site fashion, like Alaskans. Carhartt, the new Ed
Hardy! Soon, men dressed as construction workers will storm the
trendiest clubs in the big cities. The old chic brands are dead!

@Z: Northern Lights

<pic images/northern-lights-book.ps Book on the topic of the Northern Lights: 'The Northern Lights' by Syun-Ichi Akasofu.>

<em>Michael</em> 
In areas near or north of the Arctic Circle, a strange celestial
phenomenon often occurs at night: a green, sometimes red light that
seems to dance in astonishing movements, sometimes covering the sky
from horizon to horizon. This is known as the Northern Lights, or
"Aurora Borealis," a term even recognized by Americans who are not
typically fond of Latin.

As one learns in a movie screening at the Museum of the University of
Alaska in Fairbanks, and one can also read in relevant specialized books,
the phenomenon has quite complex physical causes: It is an electrical
discharge in the atmosphere, about 150 km above the Earth's surface.
The energy required for this is provided by elementary particles from
the so-called solar wind, a stream of particles emanating from the
sun's surface. When it reaches the Earth's atmosphere, the Earth's
magnetic field deflects it at the magnetic pole, creating playful
patterns. The solar wind intensifies noticeably after observable
eruptions on the sun's surface and will arrive here 2-4 days later.

So that people know what to expect, the University of Alaska in
Fairbanks provides a
<LINK>http://www.gedds.alaska.edu/AuroraForecast/|Northern Lights forecast on the internet</LINK>.
This way, the unsuspecting tourist can read on a scale from 0
to 10 on how likely it is they'll see northern lights.
Unfortunately, during our visit, the measurements
fluctuated only between 0 and 1, and the landlord of the
bed-and-breakfast accommodation informed us that these were the
lowest values in years, if not decades!

<pic images/alaska-aurora-forecast.ps The northern lights forecast for tomorrow: Cancelled due to ... it isn't!>

One night, however, I actually got up at four in the morning, dressed
warmly because of the -25 degrees outside, and even threw on a
so-called <LINK>http://www.rei.com/search?query=balaclava|Balaclava</LINK>,
a face mask previously purchased at the mountaineering store for
polar expeditions and bank robberies. With thick down mittens on
my hands, I set up a tripod outside. The camera, equipped with a
fish-eye lens, was pointed in the direction of the expected northern
lights, and I initiated a 30-second exposure. As you can see in
Figure (#1), there was absolutely no activity in the sky! Grumbling,
I trudged back to bed.

<pic images/alaska-night-sky.ps (#1) Conspicuous by its absence: The Northern Lights.>

On the second to last evening of our trip, back in Anchorage, we
observed a greenish glow as we looked towards Fairbanks from the
balcony. Due to a few patches of fog, the view was slightly
obstructed, but we believed we could even perceive gradual changes in
intensity. However, the spectacle couldn't be photographed, as the
effect was too weak. We probably need to go back to Alaska next year
to capture a proper photo!

@Z: Celsius and Fahrenheit

<pic images/alaska-temperature.ps (#3) -6 degrees Fahrenheit: -21 degrees Celsius.>

<em>Michael</em> 
Even after 13 years in America, I still can't make sense of the
Fahrenheit scale for temperatures. Well, by now I know that a
thermometer shows a body temperature of 100 degrees Fahrenheit for a
healthy patient, and that on an arctic cold winter day in Alaska, it's
0 degrees Fahrenheit.

If the pilot announces that the weather service at the destination
reports 50 degrees Fahrenheit, I put on my fleece jacket before
disembarking. At 70 degrees, a T-shirt is sufficient, and at 110
degrees, one hopes that the booked hotel has a well-functioning air
conditioning system.

That's about it. I usually set the oven to 350 degrees without
knowing how hot that actually is. And when I read in the travel guide
that on cold winter days in Alaska it can get as low as minus (!) 50
degrees Fahrenheit, I had to look it up in a table to find out that
this is minus 45 degrees Celsius. Converting it in your head isn't
quite simple, because it's not enough to just multiply a value by a
constant; you also have to subtract an offset. Roughly speaking, you
subtract 30 from the Fahrenheit value and then divide the result by
two.

<pic images/celsius-fahrenheit.ps (#2) 0 degrees Celsius correspond to (plus!) 32 degrees Fahrenheit. At -40 degrees Fahrenheit, the two scales intersect.>

When looking at the graph in Figure (#2), some striking points appear
that one might not have expected. For example, would you have known
that minus forty degrees Fahrenheit is exactly minus forty degrees
Celsius? And the -6 degrees Fahrenheit in the photo in Figure (#3),
which we took one evening in Fairbanks as we trudged through the snow
on uncleared sidewalks in our moon boots to the restaurant, can be
converted to -21 degrees Celsius.

@Z: Chena Hot Springs

<pic images/alaska-chena-ice-hair.ps Ice crystals form on the tips of your hair while sitting in a 41-degree hot pool at -25 degrees Celsius.>

<em>Angelika</em> 
In Alaska, there is not only permafrost but also the occasional
active volcano. Therefore, it is not surprising that hot water also
bubbles up from thermal springs. And lo and behold, near Fairbanks
lie the so-called Chena Hot Springs, inviting visitors to take a
leisurely dip. Conveniently, the springs are easily accessible by car
via a country road, which was even well-cleared in winter. In 1905,
two gold prospectors discovered the springs, and soon the hot water
was used for tourist purposes. Back in the day, the journey to the
springs naturally took a bit longer. Nowadays, it offers...
<LINK>http://www.chenahotsprings.com|Chena Hot Springs" is already in
English. It refers to a popular geothermal hot springs resort located
in Alaska, USA.</LINK>
In addition to the bathing fun, there are various accommodation
options and a restaurant. However, everything still has the rustic
charm of Alaska. It is by no means a chic, modern wellness center.

<pic images/alaska-pleasant-valley-hardware.ps On the way to Chena:
DIY store for cabin enthusiasts and forest hermits.>

We set off from Fairbanks with the rental car to spend a day at Chena
Hot Springs. However, it took us forever to cover the 90 kilometers
because we had to stop constantly to take photos, as there was a lot
of beautiful scenery to see.

<pic images/alaska-moose.ps A moose makes its way through the forest
in sub-zero temperatures.>

We also discovered moose by the roadside. How these huge creatures
survive the winter in Alaska remained a mystery to us, as we only
ever saw them nibbling on a few dried-up twigs. During the drive,
Michael also pondered what might happen if one were to break down on
the somewhat lonely road in the cold (it was -25 degrees Celsius) and
the engine wouldn't start, leaving us without heating. Especially at
night, when there are hardly any cars on the road, this could become
a problem.

We always had hand and foot warmers in the car with us for this
reason. These are those ingenious items that you can stick into your
gloves or onto your socks, and they provide pleasant warmth to your
feet or hands for hours through chemically generated energy. By the
way, Michael's idea wasn't so far-fetched. When we later checked into
our bed-and-breakfast in Anchorage, the owner told us that she used
to live in Fairbanks and that one of her friends lived in a house on
the way to Chena Hot Springs. One night, his car broke down in the
freezing cold on the way there. He weighed his options and then
decided to leave his car and run home to avoid freezing to death.

<pic images/alaska-chena-pool.ps Japanese tourists are outraged while
the newsletter reporter sprawls in the pool like a full-blooded
American.>

Upon arriving at Chena Hot Springs, we plugged our car into the
warming outlet (see below) and, bundled up warmly, made our way to
the bathing area and the hot springs. We paid $10 per person for our
day pass and bravely changed into our swimsuits in the changing
rooms, which reminded me of school days and swimming lessons in
indoor pools.

First, we then entered the indoor area with an ordinary swimming
pool, but the highlight at Chena Hot Springs is the outdoor pool with
41 degrees Celsius warm thermal water. However, to reach it, we had
to venture out into the cold in our swimwear. Admittedly, the path
through the open air to the hot water was perhaps 30 meters long, but
at -25 degrees Celsius, it takes quite some determination, especially
when your swimwear is wet on the way back. The hot water, of course,
immediately compensates for that. While relaxing in the water, an
amusing phenomenon occurred. The steam dampened our hair, and the
cold formed pretty ice crystals in the damp hair.

@Z: The oil pipeline from Prudhoe Bay to Valdez

<pic images/alaska-pipeline.ps The 1200 km long Alaska oil pipeline
passes by Fairbanks. To the left, it's 450 miles to Prudhoe Bay, and
to the right, it's 350 miles to Valdez.>

<em>Angelika</em> 
Alaska is not only blessed with natural beauty but also rich in
mineral resources. The discovery of oil in Prudhoe Bay at the end of
the 1960s brought the state an unexpected windfall and supports its
finances to this day. However, some environmental sins have also been
committed.

One of the most controversial projects is considered to be the
construction of the Alaska Pipeline, the approximately
1,280-kilometer-long oil pipeline that runs from the mostly frozen
Prudhoe Bay in the north across Alaska to the ice-free port of
Valdez. On one hand, the pipeline is regarded as a masterpiece of
engineering, as it not only crosses several mountain ranges and
rivers but also stands mostly on the widespread permafrost ground in
Alaska, which remains frozen both in summer and winter.

<pic images/alaska-ice.ps On bare ice: Angelika slips while getting
out of the rental car.>

On the other hand, the pipeline has always been a thorn in the side
of environmentalists and the native Indians. Environmentalists were
concerned about the intrusion into Alaska's vast untouched
landscapes, and they feared potential environmental damage from leaks
in the pipeline, as well as negative impacts on wildlife. Animal and
environmental activists were particularly worried about the survival
of the caribou herds, as they believed that the pipeline would, for
example, shorten their traditional migration routes and thus make
hunting the caribou easier.

The Native Americans complained that the pipeline ran for long
stretches over territories that rightfully belonged to them and
demanded compensation. After years of negotiations, President Nixon
finally signed the "Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act" in 1971 to
settle the dispute. The Native Americans ("Native Alaskans") received
approximately 963 million dollars and 180,000 square kilometers of
land, and in return, they allowed the construction of the pipeline.

<pic images/alaska-pipeline-pig.ps A so-called pipeline pig travels
through the pipeline and checks if everything is still functioning.>

Politically, the oil crisis of 1973 helped to push through the
construction of the pipeline. The groundbreaking took place in March
1975, and the construction of the entire pipeline was completed in
just over two years. The project brought about a gold rush
atmosphere. Cities like Fairbanks and Valdez experienced not only a
significant population increase but also steep price hikes,
especially for houses and land. The wages of the construction workers
were above average due to the harsh conditions, which led to an
increase in wage levels in other industries as well.

Long-term, the construction of the pipeline and oil production
completely eliminated the state income tax and sales tax in Alaska.
Alaska receives enough money from the oil industry to forgo these
taxes, which are common in many other states. Additionally, every
resident of Alaska receives an annual dividend from the Alaska
Permanent Fund each October.
<RBLINK>20060529#6</RBLINK>
The only question is what will happen when the oil sources run dry,
because production has already been reduced and will steadily decline
over the next 10 years.

<pic images/alaska-permafrost.ps In the permafrost, the houses are
built on stilts.>

Due to the permafrost, the pipeline runs largely above ground, as
permafrost is not only as hard as concrete, making it somewhat
difficult to dig deep into the ground, but it also has the tendency
to partially thaw when the ground warms up too much. If hot oil is
pumped through the pipeline, the ground beneath softens and causes
the pipeline to sink. Therefore, the pipeline is built in sections on
hydraulic stilts, which are designed not only to dissipate heat but
also to compensate for ground instability.

In Fairbanks, we visited a section of the pipeline that runs
conveniently along the freeway. Despite the constant fear in Alaska
that some lunatics might blow up the pipeline, everything was freely
accessible and there was not a person in sight.

@Z: Engine warmer for cold climates

<pic images/alaska-car-plugin.ps The plugin: A cable is sticking out
of the radiator hood ...>

<em>Michael</em> 
At temperatures below minus 20 degrees, a cold engine often has
trouble starting, and that's why in Alaska, a power cable from a
so-called "plugin" sticks out of the car's hood. This cable leads to
a heating element in the engine compartment, which keeps the
vehicle's radiator nice and warm. If the car also has a fairly new
battery, it will start even at minus 50 degrees.

<pic images/alaska-car-extension.ps ... which you connect to a power
outlet with an extension cord ...>

The driver plugs the connector into a regular power outlet using an
extension cord that is carried along. The flexible insulation of the
cable can withstand even the lowest temperatures without breaking,
and the car rental company charges a $35 penalty fee if it is
misplaced.

<pic images/alaska-car-running.ps ... but the Alaskan often just
leaves the engine running while he shops in the supermarket.>

However, not every parking lot offers power outlets. When we were
shopping at the "Fred Meyers" supermarket on a cold evening, about
one-fifth of the customers simply left their engines running during
their shopping trip. In the book...
<LINK>http://www.amazon.com/dp/1570614849|Real Alaskan Living</LINK>
The joke goes that you can recognize a true Alaskan by the fact that
they don't turn off their car engine for the entire month of January!

In the same book, it says that you should never admit that you have
only recently been living in Alaska, because then the seasoned, tough
guys will call you a Cheechako (pronounced "chee-chacko," with the
emphasis on "chack"), which means a newcomer or greenhorn. The word
is from the...
<LINK>http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinook_Jargon|Chinook Jargon is a
pidgin trade language that originated in the Pacific Northwest region
of North America. It was used as a lingua franca among various
Indigenous peoples, European settlers, and traders. Here are some
common Chinook Jargon words and their English translations:

- **Klahowya**: Hello - **Tenas**: Small or child - **Hyas**: Big or
very - **Muckamuck**: Food or to eat - **Tyee**: Chief or leader -
**Iktus**: Thing or object - **Halo**: No or none - **Kloshe**: Good
or well - **Lemolo**: Wild or crazy - **Tillicum**: Friend or people

If you have a specific phrase or word in Chinook Jargon that you
would like translated into English, please provide it, and I will do
my best to help.</LINK>
The term is borrowed from a linguistic mishmash that emerged in the
early 19th century in the northwest of what is now the USA, which the
first settlers used to communicate with the indigenous Native
Americans living there at the time.

Goodbye, until the next newsletter, or "klahowya" ("kla-hau-ja"), as
it's called in Chinook English!

The translation of "Angelika & Michael" to English is "Angelika &
Michael," as these are proper names and typically do not change when
