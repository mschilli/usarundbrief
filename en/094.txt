@Z: Occupy Oakland

<pic images/occupy-oakland-obama.ps Protesters in Oakland: "Obama should not be the puppet of Wall Street". Photo: flickr[David Shankbone|shankbone/6316743072]>

<em>Angelika</em> 
Surely you have already heard of the American protest movement
"Occupy Wall Street," which began in New York and spread like
wildfire to several American cities--eventually, its offshoots have
even reached Germany. I've seen this coming for a long time, for people
in the USA to finally taking to the streets, as the gap between rich
and poor is particularly wide here.

Private wealth is particularly unequally distributed here, and social
achievements such as jobs with legally regulated and paid vacation and sick
days, as well as reasonable unemployment and health insurance, leave
much to be desired compared to other Western countries. However, so
far, everyone seemed to hold on to the dream that they too would
eventually break away from the masses of the 99% and belong to the 1%
of the super-rich. Interestingly, the protests were initiated by the
right-leaning Tea Party movement. They criticized the financial
bailouts following the housing and banking crisis, as well as Obama's
push for universal healthcare. Many see the Tea Party movement as a
protest from the right and the Occupy Wall Street action as the
counter-movement from the left.

The glaring difference, however, is that the Tea Party supporters
wanted to be involved in politics from the very beginning and now
play a significant role in the American Congress. They even influence
the upcoming presidential election, while the Occupy Wall Street
group tends to keep its distance from political parties. In my
opinion, this is a big mistake, as I fear that the entire movement
might eventually dissipate. Overall, they make few concrete demands.
The common denominator seems to be opposition to big banks, which
is somewhat short-sighted, as the misery of the ongoing economic
crisis is much more complex and cannot be solved by closing accounts
at Bank of America or Wells Fargo Bank.

<pic images/occupy-oakland-city-hall.ps Protesters are occupying the plaza in front of the Oakland City Hall with tents. Photo: flickr[Clay@SU|Clay@SU/6316145786]>

Interestingly, Oakland, the city on the other side of the Bay Bridge
that always stands somewhat in the shadow of San Francisco, has
surpassed San Francisco in terms of the protest movement. "Occupy
Oakland" is receiving a lot of attention from the press, and not just
since one of the movement's sympathizers died in a shooting, which
was likely the result of a personal dispute. The protesters are still
camping in tents in front of Oakland City Hall and managed to shut
down the Port of Oakland during a large demonstration in early
November, which catapulted them into all TV channels. However,
the tents are supposed to disappear in the coming days, as Mayor Jean
Quan is increasingly under pressure. After the square in front of City
Hall was cleared by the police once before, she allowed the
protesters to settle again in their tents on Frank Ogawa Plaza.

However, the medium-sized businesses and residents around town
hall have had enough. They claim that no one dares to come to downtown
Oakland anymore and their business has dramatically decreased. Quan
hoped that the protesters would voluntarily leave. However, it was
reported this morning that riot police had cleared the square.

In general, it remains exciting to see what will become of the
movement in the various cities. To gain sustainable influence and
bring about change, it seems that protesters would need to
get involved in the political process.
I have been saying for years that the USA urgently needs a
more diverse party landscape, and much of the narrow-mindedness lies
in the currently existing two-party system.

@Z: Crazy Sales Tax

<pic images/cash-register.ps At the checkout, in addition to the sale price, stores also collect sales tax.>

<em>Michael</em> 
Inexperienced German tourists coming to the U. S. are often surprised when a
T-shirt priced at $9.99 suddenly costs $10.84 at the checkout. The
difference is due to the sales tax levied by the respective county. Similar
to how Germany uses value-added tax (VAT), the state uses this trick
to tax already taxed income again. And just like in Germany, the
seller is merely an intermediary, passing the tax collected from the
customer to the tax man without benefiting from it themselves.
Unlike in America, it would be illegal in Germany to add VAT for end
consumers only at the checkout; the price on the label already
includes VAT. Americans find it normal for the checkout to add an odd
amount.

You read it correctly before, the sales tax rate varies from county to county. If
I buy a T-shirt for $9.99, it costs $10.84 at the Macy's store in
Union Square in downtown San Francisco because the tax rate in San
Francisco County is 8.5%. However, the Macy's at the Serramonte
Shopping Mall in South San Francisco (20 km south) only charges
$10.81 because South San Francisco is in San Mateo County which sets the sales tax at
8.25%. While this may seem irrelevant to you, a man like me has to
count every penny.

<pic images/ca-sales-tax-target.ps (#2) 8.25% sales tax, but only on the sunscreen, not on the spices.>

In Figure (#2), you can see that I bought two spices and two bottles
of sunscreen at the supermarket "Target." Spices are tax-free,
whereas sunscreen is taxed at the usual rate of 8.25% in South San
Francisco. And that's not all, if you look into the
<LINK>https://www.cdtfa.ca.gov/lawguides/vol1/sutr/sales-and-use-tax-regulations.html|regulations on sales tax</LINK> which define what items are
subject to sales tax and which are not,
the typical American bureaucracy madness comes to light, which people
in Germany know nothing about.

In California, sales tax is typically not charged on groceries.
However, if a supermarket or bakery sells hot food and the customer
consumes it on a wooden bench in front of the store, the store must
charge and remit taxes as if it were a restaurant. The cashier at our
local "Whole Foods" once asked me if the leek on the conveyor belt
was intended for immediate consumption. I vehemently denied it to
avoid having to pay tax on the leek!

The different sales tax rates are calculated from the base rate for
California (7.25%) and the rate added by the county. For example, San
Francisco County charges an additional 1.25%, while South San
Francisco in San Mateo County settles for 1%. According to
<LINK>http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sales_taxes_in_the_United_States|state regulations</LINK>,
the maximum allowed sales tax rate for California is 9.75%. The county can therefore add a
maximum of 2.5% to the state's base rate. Two counties in the Los Angeles
area are going to the limit: Stores in Pico Rivera and South Gate
would charge a straight $10.95 for the $9.99 T-shirt!

Each state sets individual percentages for 
the base rate and the maximum percentage. Alaska, Delaware, New
Hampshire, Montana, and Oregon do not impose any sales tax at all and also
prohibit their counties from doing so. Additionally, the tax is only
applicable if the goods are 'consumed' in a taxable state. Also, a
customer does not have to pay sales tax on an iPod purchased in the
state and delivered to California if they immediately send it as a
gift to their ungrateful nephews in Germany.

When it comes to the previously mentioned small amounts of
sales tax when buying a T-shirt, hardly anyone cares, but if an LCD TV
suddenly costs $1,097.50 instead of $1,000, the average consumer
starts thinking and finds ways to bypass the $97.50 tax owed to the
state of California. The easiest way: order online from Amazon.
(Correction: However, that loophole has been closed since 2012).

@Z: How Amazon Circumvents Sales Tax

<pic images/online-shopping.ps Will these two crooks end up paying the California sales tax on their Amazon purchases afterwards?>

<em>Michael</em> 
The mega-retailer Amazon is headquartered near Seattle in the state
of Washington, one of the few remaining sales tax havens in America.
And since the catalog retailer Quill, which is based in the state of Delaware,
took the case
<LINK>http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quill_Corp._v._North_Dakota|Quill 1992 vs. North Dakota</LINK>
to the Supreme Court and won, it is established that companies without a branch in the
state cannot be forced to collect the local sales tax.

<pic images/amzn-sales-tax.ps Amazon is currently (as of October 2011) withholding sales tax for customers from the states of Kansas, Kentucky, New York, North Dakota, and Washington.>

This loophole is used by Amazon to avoid collecting sales
tax from active shoppers in California. For decades, it has therefore
carefully avoided establishing a physical presence in California.
Most deliveries come by truck from a warehouse near the city of Reno
on the Nevada side of the California border. And if you google
"amazon digital music san francisco," you will see, for example, job
offers at "A2Z Development Center, Inc." in the heart of San
Francisco, where software engineers are putting together Amazon's
iTunes clone. Amazon has deliberately given the department a
nondescript name so that it is not considered an Amazon branch in
California.

<pic images/jerry-brown-flickr-5403438991.ps Governor Jerry Brown wants Amazon to collect sales tax in California and fill his state's coffers. Photo: flickr [Neon Tommy|neontommy/5403438991]>

Californians are legally required to pay the saved amounts during the
annual tax reconciliation in April, but many "forget" to do so. Due
to the recession, however, the states are missing millions in their
coffers, and for several years they have been trying to force Amazon
to collect the tax. Amazon is resisting tooth and nail and can offer
its goods in California almost 10% cheaper than local stores like
Best Buy or Walmart. This, of course, infuriates the latter. If
Amazon were to run radio ads like Media Markt in Germany, the slogan
would be: "Sales tax? I'm not stupid!"

<pic images/amzn-package.ps Amazon ships packages from a warehouse outside the state of California to avoid the sales tax.>

The above-mentioned Supreme Court ruling was rendered in 1998 during
President Clinton's administration through the so-called
<LINK>http://www.cob.sjsu.edu/nellen_a/itfa.html|Federal Internet Tax Freedom Act (F-ITFA)"</LINK>.
It prohibits states
from imposing additional tax on internet purchases. However, states
are allowed to pass laws requiring online stores to
to comply with already established sales tax regulations
for regular stores. The state of New York enforced this in June 2008,
and since then, New Yorkers have been paying sales tax on Amazon
online orders.

The new tax-hungry California Governor Jerry Brown immediately took
on Amazon after his election last year. His administration targeted
the practice of so-called "Associates," where intermediaries earn a
commission on Amazon sales if the purchase is made through a link on
their website. For example, if I link to any Perl book on Amazon from
my website perlmeister.com and include an Associates ID obtained from
Amazon, I could earn up to 3% of the purchase price if someone
follows my link and buys the book.

<pic images/bezos-flickr-153327601.ps Amazon CEO Jeff Bezos wants nothing to do with sales tax. Photo: flickr[JD Lasica|jdlasica/153327601].>

If these "Associates" are Californians, Jerry
Brown found that Amazon now suddenly has a presence in the state and must
collect sales tax. Amazon immediately responded in June 2011 by
canceling the Associates program in California. The email signed by
Jeff Bezos even mentioned violations by the state of California
against the federal constitution! However, after a few weeks of
dispute, it was agreed that Amazon would collect the tax.
<LINK>http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052970204138204576598883358407422.html|From September 2012 on</LINK>
Amazon will collect California sales tax.
In early October 2011, Amazon
sent a delighted email to all terminated Associates, assuring them
that the program would now be continued. By September 2012, Amazon plans
to try to influence Congress (lobbying, see <RBLINK>20110624#2</RBLINK>)
so that a federal law puts an end to the eternal back and forth.
By the way, 2012 is the next election year.

Almost every cyclist in San Francisco wears a helmet (myself

helmet catches on. In our soccer group that plays every Tuesday in

@Z: Names and Origin

<pic images/sf-ethnicity-school.ps Parents often give their children names that reflect their heritage.>

<em>Michael</em> 
The population in San Francisco is quite diverse. As I've mentioned before in
<RBLINK>20050701#2</RBLINK>,
44% of the population here are white, 31% asian, 14%
latino, and 8% black. The map in Figure (#1) shows that members of an
'ethnicity' often concentrate in specific neighborhoods. It should
come as no surprise that mainly asians settle in Chinatown and that
predominantly whites live in affluent neighborhoods like ours in Noe
Valley. The latinos (mainly South Americans) mostly live in the
Mission district. In the southern part of the city,
there are somewhat more affordable areas where, interestingly, a
very balanced mix of skin colors can be found. The blue centers on
the map with predominantly black populations are the neighborhoods of
Tenderloin, Hunters Point, and Sunnydale, which are unfortunately
partly known as social hotspots.

<pic images/sf-ethnicity.ps (#1) Skin colors in San Francisco: red: white, blue: black, green: asian, orange: latino. Graphic: flickr[Eric Fischer|walkingsf/4981425631]>

When you meet a person for the first time and have never seen a
picture of them, most people inevitably have already formed a mental image: male or
female, old or young? If the person doesn't have an exotic name, in
Germany you wouldn't even think about their skin color, as it would
almost certainly be white.

In the USA, most people tend to infer the origin of a person they
have never seen before based on their first and last names. A "Tim
O'Reilly" is almost certainly of Irish descent, a "John McLaughlin"
is either Irish or Scottish. A "Joshua Rosenzweig" is of Jewish
descent, a "Jessica Chang" is usually Chinese, a "Srivasan
Venkataraman" is Indian. A "Manuel Rodriguez" typically comes from
South America, a "Latisha Smith" is most likely an African American,
and an "Eric Schmidt" could be a third-generation German immigrant.

<pic images/freak.ps "Freakonomics" by Steven D. Levitt and Stephen J. Dubner>

This inevitably leads to interesting questions, for example, during
the hiring process in a company: In the USA, resumes do not include a
photo to prevent discrimination based on appearance. However, a candidate's
origin often becomes apparent through the first or last name, raising
the question of which first names parents from disadvantaged
communities should choose for their children to boost their
professional careers. Should they opt for a more traditionally white
name like "Greg" or "Britney," or follow tradition with names like
"Rigoberto" or "Guadalupe" in the latino community, or "Tyrone" or
"Latisha" in the black community?

The authors of the bestseller
<LINK>http://www.amazon.com/dp/0060731338|"Freakonomics"</LINK>
(already discussed in <RBLINK>20050925#7</RBLINK>) report that
for several years now, it has been found that first names alone
cannot be directly linked to success or failure in life in a causal
way. However, it has been proven that the Tyrones and Latishas of
this world perform worse than the Gregs and Britneys. Why is that?
According to Freakonomics, the reason lies more with the parents and
the environment: someone who names their child Tyrone or Latisha
often lives in a problematic social environment and is often less
strict about checking homework compared to the parents of Greg and
Britney.

As is often the case with stereotypes, it can happen that you are
completely off the mark. The comedy series Seinfeld poked fun at this
topic a long time ago. In one episode, Jerry Seinfeld is very excited
because he has a date with a "Donna Chang," whom he only knows from
the phone. He assumes she is Asian, but is later completely surprised
when his new acquaintance turns out to be white.

@Z: Election Campaign Primaries

<em>Angelika</em> 
We find ourselves once again in the middle of an election campaign. Although
presidential elections are not until November 2012, the American
system of primaries (<RBLINK>20080203#2</RBLINK>)
has kicked off their election campaign. In the primaries, party
members themselves decide who will be sent into the presidential race
for their party. The first primaries will take place at the beginning
of 2012. Since everything is already set with the Democrats, as
only Obama is running and his nomination is just a formality, all the
focus is on the Republican candidates. The Republican base is
concerned with only one thing: Who can succeed in defeating Obama?

<pic images/mitt-romney-flickr-6149221622.ps The most promising Republican candidate, Mitt Romney. Photo: flickr [Gage Skidmore|gageskidmore/6149221622]>

Mitt Romney is considered to be the candidate with the best chances, at
least when it comes to the general presidential election and not the
primaries. Of course, this prediction is of no use to him if he
doesn't win the primaries. Herein lies the dilemma: To reach the
White House in the USA, one must appeal to the political center,
meaning garnering votes from so-called Independents. These voters do
not feel affiliated with any party and may vote for either Republican
or Democratic candidates, depending on their own mood or the mood in
the country. Generally, independent voters do not hold extreme views
and tend to penalize candidates with overly left or right political
slogans. However, to win the Republican primaries, Romney must also
appeal to the more extreme elements within his party in today's
climate, such as the religious right and the Tea Party movement.

Romney held the office of Governor of Massachusetts from 2002 to
2007 and helped introduce universal health insurance in his state.
The model in Massachusetts is very similar to the Obama model. But
since almost all Republican candidates are strictly against the
health reform initiated by Obama and even want to roll it back,
Romney is now under pressure to explain himself. He also quickly
adapted his previously moderate views on abortion and the equality of
homosexuals, opinions too moderate for some Republicans, to improve
his chances of winning. Many now accuse him of always changing his
stance with the prevailing winds. On the plus side, many see that
Romney was a successful businessman before he showed political
ambitions, leading some companies out of trouble and bringing the
2002 Winter Olympics to Salt Lake City. Many hope that he will be
able to tackle the national debt and high unemployment. However,
Romney has little experience in foreign policy. His opponents also
repeatedly bring up the fact that Romney is a Mormon. However, this
seems to carry less weight than it did in 2008, when Romney ran
before but McCain emerged as the winner in the primaries.

<pic images/herman-cain-flickr-6256703238.ps For only 9 percent income tax: The Republican Herman Cain. Photo: flickr [Gage Skidmore|gageskidmore/6256703238]>

Another dazzling personality is Herman Cain, who initially was not
given any chances by anyone, but is now performing relatively well in
polls. The former CEO of "Godfather's Pizza" is known for his
pragmatic approaches and is considered a charismatic speaker. A man
of the people, coming from very humble beginnings, who has made
something of himself. Americans love such stories. His much-quoted
proposed tax reform "9-9-9," which experts consider completely
unfeasible, is nevertheless appealing to many, because Americans hate
the complicated, convoluted tax laws with their associated
exceptions.
<LINK>http://www.hermancain.com/999plan|Herman Cain's Plan</LINK>
includes a flat tax rate of only 9%, hence the name.
Everyone pays 9% income tax, a national sales tax of 9% is
introduced, and businesses are also taxed at 9%. However, Cain
currently has other problems, as the press dug up an old story from
the 1990s when he was the head of the National Restaurant Association
and three women filed complaints against him for sexual harassment in
the workplace. He paid a settlement to one of the affected
individuals at the time. Sexual harassment in the workplace is
handled quite strictly in America, and it can range from
inappropriate jokes among colleagues to more serious offenses. This
is also typical of American election campaigns: the press and the
opposition dig into the candidates' lives until they find something
that can harm the candidate and cost them votes.

The Texan Rick Perry would like to follow in the footsteps of George
W. Bush once again, as he took over the office of Texas governor from
Bush in 2000 and now hopes for the presidency. The son of a cattle
rancher often boasts that he initiated a true economic boom in Texas
by creating jobs despite an economic downturn in the rest of the
country. However, Texas is a unique place, and what works well there
doesn't necessarily work in the rest of the country. In the first
debates, Perry stepped into several pitfalls, as he often mixed up
facts and spoke freely without thinking about what he was saying
beforehand. He strictly opposes Obama's healthcare reform, which
leads Perry's critics to repeatedly point out that under his
leadership, Texas set the unfortunate record of having the highest
percentage of uninsured citizens at 26%.

<pic images/michelle-bachman-flickr-6254644721.ps The second Sarah Palin: Michele Bachmann. Photo: flickr[Gage Skidmore|gageskidmore/6254644721]>

Michele Bachmann was long considered the second Sarah Palin and
received a lot of attention from the press before Rick Perry
announced his ambitions for the presidency. This was likely also
because, like Palin, she often throws around conservative political
platitudes. She advocates for low taxes, limited government, is
against healthcare reform, against same-sex partnerships and
marriages, and believes that climate change is a myth. She is
affiliated with the Tea Party movement and has been serving in the
U.S. House of Representatives since 2007 as one of the
representatives from the state of Minnesota.

<pic images/ron-paul-flickr-5449859622.ps Translation: "Wants to abolish the US Federal Reserve: the Republican Ron Paul. Photo: flickr[Gage Skidmore|gageskidmore/5449859622]>

Ron Paul is a doctor and a Texas representative in the House
of Representatives. He is considered a free thinker with principles
that seem somewhat utopian. His goal is to strictly adhere to the
principles of the nation's founding fathers. He wants to keep the
government as small as possible, believes in self-governance and
deregulation, the simplification of the tax system, the abolition of
the Federal Reserve, a significant reduction of the American
military, and a foreign policy of non-intervention. Many see him as a
pioneer of the Tea Party movement, although not all of his principles
align with the movement. He has loyal followers who do not only come
from the right-wing camp, but he is considered an outsider, as he is
unlikely to become president with these principles, even in extreme
America. However, the man is consistent. For example, he stubbornly
refuses to accept the pension payments available to Congress
members at a certain age and after years of service.

Jon Huntsman may be the only Republican who could also find support
among Europeans. He is considered a moderate (at least by American
standards and in comparison to the other candidates) and is the only
candidate with foreign policy experience. Most recently, he served as
the U.S. Ambassador to China from 2009 to 2011, under the Obama
administration. He worked in various roles under Ronald Reagan,
George H.W. Bush, and George W. Bush and was an extremely popular
governor in Utah. Like Romney, he is also an openly practicing
Mormon.

Newt Gingrich had his best political years in the mid-1990s, when he
played a significant role in helping the Republicans gain a majority
in the U.S. House of Representatives in 1994 after a long dry spell.
From 1995 to 1999, he served as the Speaker of the House. He is
considered an extremely polarizing politician with a not entirely
spotless personal record, which is a thorn in the side of many of his
party colleagues. He is married for the third time and, while he was
pushing for Bill Clinton's impeachment over the Lewinsky affair, he
himself had an extramarital affair with a staff member, whom he later
married.

Some candidates are now considered completely without a chance and
often do not even appear in political debates: Rick Santorum, a
former senator from Pennsylvania; Buddy Roemer, a former Democrat who
not only switched parties but also served as governor of Louisiana
and sat in the House of Representatives; and the former governor of
New Mexico, Gary Johnson, who attempted to legalize marijuana in New
Mexico. A surprisingly liberal move for a Republican.

There is no shortage of candidates. However, when the actual
primaries finally begin, the wheat is quickly separated from the
chaff, because election campaignsare expensive, and many candidates
only make it to the primaries in the initial states. Candidates find it
difficult to raise campaign funds if they are not leading in the
primaries. Two other candidates, Thaddeus McCotter and Tim Pawlenty,
already dropped out in August and September for this reason.

@Z: Counting With Fingers

<pic images/count-three.ps (#13) This is how you signal the number three in American.>

<em>Michael</em> 
At the latest since the Tarantino film
<LINK>http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inglourious_Basterds|"Inglourious Basterds"</LINK>
in which an American spy gives himself away by ordering three beers
with the wrong fingers, it should be clear that Americans and Germans
use different finger signs.

The number one is signaled by Americans with an extended index
finger, and for the number two, they form a peace sign with the index
and middle fingers. In Germany, for the number one, people raise
their thumb and add the index finger for the number two.

Things get really crazy with the number three: While Germans extend
their thumb, index, and middle fingers, Americans use their index,
middle, and ring fingers. This extremely challenging gesture can only
be accomplished by ordinary people, who don't moonlight as
contortionists in the circus, by crossing their little finger with
their thumb and holding both firmly at the bottom (see illustration
(#13)).

And what does it mean when you only raise the index finger and the
little finger, while keeping the middle and ring fingers folded down?
Correct, that is the signal for "Four beers for the men from the
sawmill", of course!

@Z: The End Of The Paper Book

<pic images/kindle-sarrazin.ps On the Kindle, you can read just like in a book.>

<em>Michael</em> 
As an avid newsletter reader, you've already read in
<RBLINK>20110320#5</RBLINK>
about the revolution of the electronic Amazon book readers of the
"Kindle" brand.
In Germany, too, they seem
to be catching on quickly; the German Amazon site offers a decent
selection of contemporary titles.

In my opinion, this development heralds a rapid end to all printed
materials and bookstores. Soon, schoolchildren will incredulously
point at people with traditional books, as if they were egocentrics
listening to records with a needle. All publishers that do not
promptly adapt will sooner or later have to declare bankruptcy. As
always, you hear it first in the newsletter, from your reporters from
the future. Those who are smart will listen, act accordingly, and
swiftly convert their publications to the new electronic format.

The advantages are obvious: Not only do you no longer have to lug
around a kilo of books on vacation. If I need a page from a 1000-page
Unix tome, I have it with me always and everywhere. New reading
material can be found directly on the reading device, and you can
purchase it right there without any hassle, without even having to get up
from the deck chair on the beach or the toilet seat. The transfer is
wireless and free via the mobile network within a few minutes.
Magazines are no longer subscribed to by mail; Amazon automatically
delivers them to the reading device as soon as they are published.

For some time now, I have been reading the SZ-Magazin on the iPad. On
Thursday morning (thanks to the time difference), the new issue is
available, and for $0.99, the elaborately produced print work is

In Germany, people are still finding it difficult to take this
development seriously. I sometimes hear excuses like "Germany is not
America" and "the good book will never disappear," but recently,
fundamental conditions have changed that will dramatically accelerate
the once hesitant development. Many Germans compare the eBook to the
onset of the internet age, where, as is well known, practically no
one managed to sell a publication online at a profit without
plastering it with advertisements.

However, the hesitant ones overlook a very important difference
compared to back then: While a free culture prevails on the internet
and it is considered uncool to spend money on well-processed and
presented content, most products on the Kindle or iPad do cost money,
albeit not much, but real money. It is normal to pay small amounts
for music or apps. Even the hacker magazine "2600," which I admire,
came out with a Kindle edition some time ago and recently reported
that in less than a year, it has gained 10,000 new subscribers who
pay $0.99 a month for a quarterly Kindle edition!

The SZ-Magazin recently reported that Kindle books are now also being
pirated, but I don't believe this will become widespread. However,
this is only the case if publishers are smart enough to set fair
prices: Who would go through the trouble of downloading a pirated
copy if the official book costs only a few euros?

<pic images/kindle-on-loan.ps (#14) If you have borrowed a Kindle book, it is locked on your own Kindle.>

The Kindle amusingly also models established habits of traditional
book readers: For example, you can lend a purchased book to a friend
who owns a Kindle for two weeks. During this period, the copy remains
locked on the lender's Kindle (Figure (#14)). Libraries can also
purchase a number of copies of a digital book and lend them to their
users. The
<LINK>http://www.jacksonfuller.com/2011/09/21/san-francisco-public-library-and-kindle-lending-my-personal-experience|Main branch of the San Francisco Public Library at the Civic Center now also lends
Kindle books</LINK>.
However, enthusiastic library users seem to have already cleared out the digital
shelves, the quota seems to be exhausted, and people have signed up
for waiting lists. It's actually a bizarre situation: The edition of
a digital book, of which one could make an infinite number of copies
for free, is artificially limited to mimic the constraints of printed
publications.

<pic images/sfpl-civic-center.ps The venerable San Francisco Public Library also lends electronic Kindle books.>

Conveniently, the Kindle remembers how far you've flipped through
each book, so you never read a page twice, even if you're juggling 20
books at once. And even better: The Kindle apps on different devices do
automatically sync, so if I'm reading a Kindle book at home on the
iPad and later turn on a Kindle device on the bus, the Kindle asks if
it should fast forward to where I left off on the iPad. The ability
to highlight sentences and write notes in the margins, with this data
also being available on other reading devices later, completes the
package.
